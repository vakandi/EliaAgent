# Startup Playbook Skill

## Description
Navigate the messy reality of building companies with Alex Hormozi's playbook. Learn why the fastest growth is often miserable, how to manage the risk of new, and why "the time you do new is when you start" - after that, everything is about more and better.

## Core Principles

### 1. The Business You're In Is Temporary
> "The business that you're in right now is probably not the one you're gonna end up in."

Your first business teaches you lessons. Those lessons build the real business. Be open to the pivot.

### 2. Growth and Misery Are Correlated
> "The fastest growth periods are often the most miserable."

Rapid growth exposes every weakness simultaneously. If it's hard, you're probably doing it right.

### 3. Elite Athletes Embrace Discomfort
> "Elite athletes don't get stronger during easy workouts."

If you're comfortable, you're not growing. Progress requires stress, recovery, adaptation.

### 4. Progress Requires Sacrifice
> "You cannot both want progress and live an easy life."

Every meaningful outcome requires tradeoffs. The question isn't whether to trade, it's what to trade for what.

### 5. The Risk of New Is Misjudged
> "The risk of new is one of the most misjudged risks in business."

We fear starting more than we should and fear staying less than we should.

### 6. Start = New, Then = More/Better
> "The time you do new is when you start - after that, everything is almost more or better."

The hard part is starting. Once you've started, the work becomes optimization, not invention.

## Actionable Frameworks

### Framework 1: The Startup Survival Framework

```
PHASE 1: SURVIVAL (Months 1-6)
Goal: Get to positive cash flow
- Focus: ONE product, ONE channel, ONE customer
- Measure: Are people paying?
- Avoid: Premature scaling, shiny objects
- Key: Get one thing working before adding complexity

PHASE 2: VALIDATION (Months 6-12)
Goal: Prove unit economics
- Focus: Can I acquire customers profitably?
- Measure: CAC < LTV, positive margins
- Avoid: Growth at all costs without economics
- Key: Double down on what works, kill what doesn't

PHASE 3: GROWTH (Months 12-24)
Goal: Scale what works
- Focus: Outlaw the parts that don't scale
- Measure: Revenue growth rate, efficiency
- Avoid: Complex processes that worked at small scale
- Key: Systemize, hire, delegate

PHASE 4: BUILDING (Months 24+)
Goal: Create leverage
- Focus: Systems that work without you
- Measure: Revenue per employee, automation %  
- Avoid: Founder dependency
- Key: Replace yourself with systems, then with people
```

### Framework 2: The "When to Quit Your Day Job" Checklist

```
FINANCIAL CHECKLIST:
[ ] 6 months of personal expenses saved
[ ] Health insurance secured
[ ] Have 2+ months of runway from business revenue
[ ] Have a clear "number" that triggers going back

PROOF OF CONCEPT CHECKLIST:
[ ] Have paying customers (not just interest)
[ ] Have tested the acquisition channel
[ ] Know the unit economics work
[ ] Have a plan to get to profitability

SUPPORT SYSTEM CHECKLIST:
[ ] Partner/family aligned on the decision
[ ] Have accountability partner or mentor
[ ] Have a "when to quit" plan for the business too

MOTIVATION CHECKLIST:
[ ] Intrinsic motivation (not ego/fear)
[ ] Clear why that matters to you
[ ] Can imagine the pain of NOT trying

DECISION: If all boxes checked, you're ready to bet on yourself.
```

### Framework 3: The Risk Assessment Matrix

```
FOR ANY NEW VENTURE, ASSESS:

CAPITAL RISK (How much money can I lose?)
Best case: [X]
Worst case: [Y]
Survivable: YES/NO

TIME RISK (How much time can I waste?)
Best case: [X months to validation]
Worst case: [Y months]
Survivable: YES/NO

OPPORTUNITY RISK (What else could I be doing?)
Best case: [This succeeds]
Worst case: [Lost time doing other thing]
Better elsewhere: YES/NO

REPUTATION RISK (Can my name survive failure?)
Best case: [Reputation boost]
Worst case: [Reputation hit]
Recoverable: YES/NO

RELATIONSHIP RISK (Will it hurt my relationships?)
Best case: [Partner supports]
Worst case: [Partner leaves]
Work-through-able: YES/NO

IF ALL YES → DO IT
IF MOSTLY YES → REDUCE SCOPE AND DO IT
IF MOSTLY NO → DON'T DO IT (or wait)
```

## Scripts & Templates

### Script 1: The First Customer Pitch

```
"Hi [Name],

I know you're busy, so I'll be direct.

I'm building [product/service] that helps [target customer] who want [specific outcome].

We've already helped [X] customers achieve [result].

I'm looking for people to test this with before we launch publicly.

Would you be open to a 20-minute call this week to see if this would be valuable for you?

No pitch, just conversation. If it's not a fit, I'll tell you honestly."

KEY: First customers are doing you a favor. Overdeliver. Get testimonials. Get specific feedback.
```

### Script 2: The Pivot Announcement

```
[To team/investors/customers]

"I want to share an update on our direction.

When we started, we believed [original thesis].

What we've learned: [What the data/market/customers told us]

Based on this, we're pivoting to [new direction].

Why this is good:
- [Reason 1]
- [Reason 2]
- [Reason 3]

What this means for you:
- [Specific impact]
- [Timeline]
- [What we need from them]

I'm confident this is the right move. The lessons from [old direction] are directly applicable to [new direction].

Questions?"
```

### Script 3: The "Should I Continue" Reflection

```
Ask yourself honestly:

1. "Am I still excited about this when I wake up?"
   - Yes: Keep going
   - No: Find the root cause (burnout vs. wrong thing)

2. "Is the market responding?"
   - Getting easier to acquire customers: Keep going
   - Getting harder: Diagnose why

3. "Can I see a path to my goals?"
   - Yes, with clear milestones: Keep going
   - Maybe, fuzzy path: Get clarity first
   - No: Consider pivot or pause

4. "If I quit today, would I regret it?"
   - Yes: Keep going
   - No: Quit with clear head

5. "Am I the right person for this?"
   - Yes: Double down
   - No: Find the right person or pivot to something where you are
```

## Real Examples

### Example 1: The "Right Business, Wrong Time" Pivot
A founder spent 3 years building a B2C app. Excellent product, 2,000 users, zero monetization. Ran out of money. Pivoted to B2B licensing the same technology. Turned into $5M ARR business. Same core technology, completely different business model. The lessons about the tech transferred; the initial thesis didn't.

### Example 2: The Growth = Misery Truth
A DTC brand hit $1M/month revenue in 8 months. Founder was miserable. Team was burning out. Systems were breaking. Cash flow was stressed. It looked like "success" from outside but felt like chaos inside. The fix: Slowed growth intentionally, fixed operations, rebuilt culture. 18 months later: Same revenue, founder happy, team stable, profitable.

### Example 3: The "More/Better" Evolution
A consultant started doing one-on-one coaching. Then hired one coach. Then systematized the process. Then created group program. Then licensed the curriculum. Same core offer (business coaching), evolved seven times. Each evolution was "more and better," not "new." The business grew because he kept finding leverage, not because he kept starting over.

## Anti-Patterns (What NOT to Do)

1. **Don't scale before validating** - Prove unit economics at small scale before investing in growth.

2. **Don't fall in love with your first idea** - The business you're in now isn't the one you'll end up in.

3. **Don't ignore early warning signs** - If the market isn't responding, diagnose early.

4. **Don't compare your chapter 2 to someone else's chapter 20** - Everyone's timeline is different.

5. **Don't let growth outpace your systems** - Fast growth that breaks everything isn't growth, it's chaos.

6. **Don't start things that require you to be someone you're not** - Build from your strengths.

7. **Don't stay in businesses that no longer serve you** - The lessons you learned are transferable even if the business isn't.
